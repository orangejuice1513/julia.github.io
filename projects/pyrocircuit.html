Pyrocircuit

The pyrocircuit takes a digital input signal from the MCU on the flight computer in order to light an e-match and deploy the main and drogue parachutes.

I experimented with an analog pyrocircuit using an NMOS switch, which allows the required 1 A current to flow through the e-match when the MCU pin goes high. I iterated through multiple designs, each with their own merits: least component count, single-fault tolerant, and double-fault tolerant. All designs include a voltage divider that senses when the IGNITE signal is sent and whether the e-match is connected.

  • 2.0 The system shall require a physical arming switch to be in the "ARM" position to enable pyro channel outputs.
  • 2.1 The high-power input from the flight battery shall be protected from overcurrent or short-circuit events by a user-serviceable fuse.
  • 2.2 The power rail shall include bulk capacitance to supply high-transient current demands during ignition and maintain voltage stability.
  • 2.3 Default Safe State: The pyro channel shall default to OFF if the logic input is disconnected, floating, or high-impedance (e.g., during boot-up).
  • 2.4 Flight Computer Protection: The logic input shall be high-impedance (e.g., a MOSFET gate) to avoid loading the GPIO pin.
  • 2.5 The logic input shall include filtering to reject high-frequency noise transients.
  • 2.6 The system shall provide a visual indicator (LED) that illuminates when the logic-high signal is present.
  • 2.7 The system shall provide a logic-level feedback signal to the flight computer indicating the armed rail is active.
  • 2.8 Any sense-lines connected to the high-voltage rail shall be scaled within the safe input voltage tolerance of the GPIO pins.
  • 2.9 The pyro channel shall be controlled by two independent, series-switched solid-state devices.
    • 2.9.1 These switches shall be driven by separate, independent logic signals to protect against single-point failures.
  • 2.10 The system shall be protected from damage or inadvertent operation from reverse-polarity battery connection.

Considerations

  • System power-up shall not induce current exceeding the 0.3 A no-fire threshold. Miller capacitance on the NMOS can be mitigated with a high-side PMOS.
  • High-impedance/floating MCU GPIO pins during initialization or reset shall not trigger firing.
  • Single-Fault Tolerance: Circuit remains open and safe if any single component fails.
  • Two-Fault Tolerance: At least two independent actions required to fire — arming switch plus MCU signal.
  • Continuous e-match continuity sensing must stay strictly below the 0.3 A no-fire current.
  • No-Fire Limit: All transient and diagnostic currents < 0.3 A under non-deployment conditions.
  • All-Fire Requirement: Upon authorized MCU deployment signal, circuit reliably sources and sustains > 1 A.
  • Battery Sag: Voltage droop causing MCU reset or gate-driver undefined states must keep MOSFET current < 0.3 A.

Design 6 — Added Diode Clamps

Added diode clamps (BAT54) to protect MCU pins from voltage spikes. Moved to KiCad schematic with real component footprints (FD6673BZ PMOS, AO3400A NMOS). Problem: doesn't protect the SENSE_IO pin from 12 V when firing the e-match.

Design 7 — Final

Final design with proper e-match continuity sensing, MCU pin protection (BAT54 diode clamps on sense and ignite lines), pullup/pulldown resistors for safe default states, and transient protection capacitors. Uses FD6673BZ PMOS, AO3400A NMOS, and 1812L150/24MR resettable fuse.
